Abstract

Health insurance coverage is an important factor for individuals residing in the United States to access a variety of preventative and treatment services in medical setting (Office of Disease Prevention and Promotion 2025). The current study examines the variations of health insurance status across demographic characteristics including race, ethnicity, age, sex, employment, income using Wave V of ADD Health survey data. Introduction

Access to health services has been identified as a social determinant of health by the Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion (Office of Disease Prevention and Promotion 2025). Status on health insurance coverage is a factor that largely contributes to accessing healthcare services, for both treatment and preventative services. Many adverse outcomes can result from a lack of health insurance coverage. Uninsured adults are less likely to receive preventative screenings for chronic conditions, such as cancer, cardiovascular disease, and diabetes, resulting in higher mortality among uninsured groups due to untimely diagnoses and treatments (Medicine (U.S.) Committee on Health Insurance Status and Consequences 2009). In addition, there’s a higher likelihood of medical debt among uninsured groups due to accrued out-of-pocket expenses (Herman, Rissi, and Walsh 2011).

Access to health insurance coverage is unequally distributed across people with lower incomes, racial/ethnic minorities,gender minorities, and young adults in the United States (Hadley 2003). Particularly, disparities across health insurance coverage primarily affect people in lower-income classes or minoritized racial and ethnic groups, as about half of the uninsured population in the U.S. are also in lower-income classes or identified as belonging to a minoritized racial and ethnic group (Majerol, Newkirk, and Garfield 2015). As the U.S. population has become more racially and ethnically diverse and more individuals identified as multiracial (Jones 2021), it is crucial to understand whether there are any variations in monoracial and multiracial individuals’ access to health insurance and services. The current study aims to examine the distribution of health insurance coverage and how this may differ by race (monoracial, multiracial), ethnicity, age, sex, income and employment using ADD Health Wave 5 survey data.

Methods

The current study uses the public available version of the ADD Health Wave 5 survey data, which was collected from 2016 through 2018. As the full dataset contains variables on many social, environmental, behavior and biological measures beyond the scope of the current study, we only selected variables that are relevant to our aim as a first step of our data cleaning process. We used the following R packages (R Core Team 2025; Müller 2020; Wickham et al. 2019; Chan et al. 2023; Firke 2024; Iannone et al. 2025) to assist our data cleaning, as well as table and visualization creations of our results.

As we are interested in examining the potential differences in health insurance coverage by comparing monoracial and multiracial individuals that either identifies as Hispanic or Non-Hispanic rather than across each racial and ethnic group, we decided to exclude participants who have missing data on race and ethnicity. Therefore, our final sample includes 4184 adult participants (Mean age =37.35 years, Range = 32 - 43 years).

Discussion

Findings about Gender and Employment

The descriptive table 1 and figures 1 highlight differences in employment status by gender and how it is linked to health insurance coverage. In this sample and analysis of Gender and Employment table, women have higher raw counts of employment because the female sample is larger.However, men shows the higher employment rate (87.3%) compared to women (79.6%). Women also have a higher count of “not employed” but proportionally still lower than men. Since there are small NA counts, it suggests that there will be minimal missing data and do not affect the patterns that much. This table of Gender and Employment status reflects labor-force disparities in U.S. It suggests slight gender differences in employment stability or participation rates.

The figure 1: Employment Status by Gender visualizes the same pattern that both genders are mostly employed. The bar chart visually shows larger absolute employment counts for women. However, proportionally, men are more likely to be employed when sample size is considered. It indicates gender differences in labor market participation in this sample. This analysis highlights the importance of distingushing between absolute counts and percentage. Employment is tightly linked to employer-sponsored insurance, so even small gender differences can influence coverage patterns.

Implications for Health Insurance

Higher male employment rates explain why men may have greater access to employer-based health insurance. Since women have relatively lower employment rate, it may increase reliance on non-employer insurance options such as Medicaid and partner insurance. This is because U.S. insurance is heavily employment-dependent, gendered labor-market patterns can translate directly into coverage disparities. These findings align with broader literature on women’s higher likelihood of part-time work or employment interruptions which may limit access to employer benefits.

###Findings about Job Type Job types vary significantly across participants and private for-profit employment is the most common one. Government jobs such as local, state and federal also represent meaningful shares of the sample. Self-employed and unpaid family workers represents smaller but important groups.

###Insurance Access Patterns Private for-profit and government job types show the highest rates of employer-sponsored insurance while self-employed individuals have much lower access to employer insurance and rely more on individual market plans or remin uninsured. Unpaid or informal job types show very low employer-insurance coverage and higher Medicaid and uninsured rates. Therefore, job type is a strong structural determinant of insurance access even among people who are technically “employed”.

Implications

Employer-provided insurance is unequally distributed across job sectors. Workers in traditional and stable employment sectors such as governments or for-profit benefit from higher coverage. Workers in self-employment or informal roles face systemic disadvantages in getting decent insurance. This support the argument that the structure of the labor market creates disparities in health insurance acess beyond employment status alone.
