Understanding demographic variations in health insurance coverage in the United States: evidence from ADD Health

EDLD 651 Final Paper

Author

Sara Kiros, Ohnmar Khine Min, Chen Su

Abstract

Health insurance coverage is an important factor for individuals residing in the United States to access a variety of preventative and treatment services in medical settings (Office of Disease Prevention and Promotion 2025). The current study examines the variations of health insurance status across demographic characteristics including race, ethnicity, age, sex, employment, income using Wave V of ADD Health survey data (Harris and Udry 2022). We found that lower-income classes had higher rated of being uninsured and the primary provider of health insurance was through the workplace across all income levels. These findings highlights the importance of addressing disparities in health insurance coverage to improve access to care for diverse population, which is crucial for the successful prevention and management of chronic health conditions for all.

Introduction

Access to health services has been identified as a social determinant of health by the Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion (Office of Disease Prevention and Promotion 2025). Status on health insurance coverage is a factor that largely contributes to accessing healthcare services, for both treatment and preventative services. Many adverse outcomes can result from a lack of health insurance coverage. Uninsured adults are less likely to receive preventative screenings for chronic conditions, such as cancer, cardiovascular disease, and diabetes, resulting in higher mortality among uninsured groups due to untimely diagnoses and treatments (Medicine (U.S.) Committee on Health Insurance Status and Consequences 2009). In addition, there’s a higher likelihood of medical debt among uninsured groups due to accrued out-of-pocket expenses (Herman, Rissi, and Walsh 2011).

Access to health insurance coverage is unequally distributed across people with lower incomes, racial/ethnic minorities,gender minorities, and young adults in the United States (Hadley 2003). Particularly, disparities across health insurance coverage primarily affect people in lower-income classes or minoritized racial and ethnic groups, as about half of the uninsured population in the U.S. are also in lower-income classes or identified as belonging to a minoritized racial and ethnic group (Majerol, Newkirk, and Garfield 2015). As the U.S. population has become more racially and ethnically diverse and more individuals identified as multiracial (Jones 2021), it is crucial to understand whether there are any variations in monoracial and multiracial individuals’ access to health insurance and services. The current study aims to examine the distribution of health insurance coverage and how this may differ by race (monoracial, multiracial), ethnicity, age, sex, income and employment using ADD Health Wave 5 survey data (Harris and Udry 2022).

Methods

The current study uses the public available version of the ADD Health Wave 5 survey data (Harris and Udry 2022), which was collected from 2016 through 2018. As the full dataset contains variables on many social, environmental, behavior and biological measures beyond the scope of the current study, we only selected variables that are relevant to our aim as a first step of our data cleaning process. We used the following R packages (R Core Team 2025; Müller 2020; Wickham et al. 2019; Chan et al. 2023; Firke 2024; Iannone et al. 2025) to assist our data cleaning, as well as (Wickham 2016, 2023; Wickham, Pedersen, and Seidel 2023; Wickham et al. 2023) for table and visualization creations of our results.

As we are interested in examining the potential differences in health insurance coverage by comparing monoracial and multiracial individuals that either identifies as Hispanic or Non-Hispanic rather than across each racial and ethnic group, we decided to exclude participants who have missing data on race and ethnicity. Therefore, our final sample includes 4184 adult participants (Mean age =37.35 years, Range = 32 - 43 years).

Additionally, we are interested in examining differences in status of health insurance and types of health insurance across income levels. The original raw data had thirteen income levels, starting at "Less than $5,000", increasing in increments of $5,000 up to "$200,000 or more". These income levels from the raw data were collapsed to create better visualizations that are more clear and interpretable. For our final income levels, participants were grouped into earning:Below 50k,50k-100k,100k-200k, andAbove 200k.

Results

We presented descriptive statistics of our demographic variables of interest in Table 1 below. The majority of our participants are employed, reported their household income as above 50k and their sex assigned at birth as female, and identified as Non-Hispanic Monoracial (see Table 1).The primary reason behind not having health insurance was it being too expensive at 55.1% (see Table 5). The largest proportion of insurance types across the four income levels was receiving health insurance through the workplace (see Figure 5). However, the proportions of receiving insurance through the workplace was lower at both the lowest and highest end of income levels, Below 50k and Above 200k. The second leading insurance type was “Medicaid” for those in the Below 50k category, and “Partner’s Insurance” in the rest of the categories.

For each racial ethnic group, we found that the majority of people receive health insurance coverage through work. However, a larger proportion of Multiracial individuals across ethnicity are more likely to have no health insurance when compared to Monoracial individuals. Interestingly, while a larger proportion of Hispanic Monoracial individuals have no health insurance when compared to Non-Hispanic Monoracial individuals, we found this to be in the opposite direction for Multiracial individuals.

In this sample and analysis of Sex and Employment, women have higher raw counts of employment because the female sample is larger. However, men shows the higher employment rate (87.3%) compared to women (79.6%). Women also have a higher count of “not employed” but proportionally still lower than men. Since there are small NA counts, it suggests that there will be minimal missing data but do not affect the patterns that much. This table of Sex and Employment status reflects labor-force disparities in U.S. It suggests slight differences in employment stability or participation rates based on the participants’ sex.

Discussion

Findings about Sex and Employment

Employment Status by sex shows the same pattern that both men and women are mostly employed. It demonstrates larger absolute employment counts for women. However, proportionally, men are more likely to be employed when sample size is considered. It indicates sex differences in labor market participation in this sample. This analysis highlights the importance of distinguishing between absolute counts and percentage. Employment is tightly linked to employer-sponsored insurance, so even small differences in participants’ sex can influence coverage patterns.

Findings about Job Type

Job types vary significantly across participants and private for-profit employment is the most common one. Government jobs such as local, state and federal also represent meaningful shares of the sample. Self-employed and unpaid family workers represents smaller but important groups.

Insurance Access Patterns

By looking into Figure 4, Private for-profit and government job types show the highest rates of employer-sponsored insurance while self-employed individuals have much lower access to employer insurance and rely more on individual market plans or remain uninsured. Unpaid or informal job types show very low employer-insurance coverage and higher Medicaid and uninsured rates. Therefore, job type is a strong structural determinant of insurance access even among people who are technically “employed”.

Implications

Employer-provided insurance is unequally distributed across job sectors. Workers in traditional and stable employment sectors such as governments or for-profit benefit from higher coverage. Workers in self-employment or informal roles face systemic disadvantages in getting decent insurance. Unemployed persons face higher risks of having no health insurance coverage due to systemic reliance on employer-sponsored health insurance models. Those with lower incomes also face higher risks for having no health insurance coverage, thus, leading to higher risks of delaying health services or prioritizing preventative care. This support the argument that the structure of the labor market creates disparities in health insurance access beyond employment status alone.

Findings about Income levels

Figure 4 revealed that receiving health insurance through the workplace remains the primary reason across all income levels. In addition, the largest proportion of “No Health Insurance” responses was in the lowest income level, however was partially mitigated by Medicaid. These figure findings highlight the importance in altering employer reliant healthcare insurance models, as federally provided health insurance, such as Medicaid, can clearly aid in drastically reducing uninsured statuses.

Findings about Reasons for No Health Insurance

Table 2 revealed that the primary reason behind not having health insurance was due to it being too expensive or costly (55.1%). These results shed light on how cost/affordability continues to be major barrier to health insurance status and accessing health services. The second leading reason of not having health insurance, “Not offered via work or school”(38.5%), convey the risks from relying heavily on workplaces to provide health insurance, which is the gap of leaving unemployed persons at a higher risk of being uninsured, based on their employment status. These results can be used to support universal healthcare policies by highlighting existing health care disparities stemming from these flawed healthcare systems.

Implications for Health Insurance Coverage

In the current study, we examined the distribution of health insurance coverage within each racial and ethnic group of interest (Non-Hispanic Monoracial, Hispanic Monoracial, Non-Hispanic Multiracial, Hispanic Multiracial). We found variations in health insurance coverage types for individuals of each racial and ethnic group. While Multiracial individuals make up a small proportion of the current study sample, there’s emerging evidence that they are the most vulnerable population in terms of having health insurance coverage. Future studies should examine health insurance and coverage for Multiracial individuals in the United States.

Higher male employment rates explain why men may have greater access to employer-based health insurance. Since women have relatively lower employment rate, it may increase reliance on non-employer insurance options such as Medicaid and partner insurance. This is because U.S. insurance is heavily employment-dependent, gendered labor-market patterns can translate directly into coverage disparities. These findings align with broader literature on women’s higher likelihood of part-time work or employment interruptions which may limit access to employer benefits.
